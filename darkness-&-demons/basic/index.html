Introduction

What Is Darkness & Demons Basic?

Darkness & Demons Basic is a streamlined fantasy role‑playing game designed to be easy to learn, fast to play, and welcoming to anyone stepping into tabletop adventures for the first time. It keeps the heart of classic d20 fantasy—heroes, monsters, magic, and danger—but presents it in a clear, modern style that avoids complicated rules and heavy jargon. The focus is on smooth play, quick decisions, and rules that support imagination rather than getting in its way.

At its core, the game is about a group of players creating heroes who explore mysterious places, battle fearsome creatures, and make choices that shape the world around them. One player takes the role of the Game Master, describing the world and guiding the story, while the others act as the heroes within it. Every challenge—whether swinging a sword, sneaking past a guard, or casting a spell—is resolved with a simple roll of a twenty‑sided die.

Darkness & Demons Basic uses a clean three‑action turn system and straightforward character abilities, making it easy to understand what your hero can do at any moment. Characters grow from level 1 to level 20, gaining new powers and spells along the way, but the rules stay focused and approachable throughout. Whether you’re coming from Pathfinder or Dungeons & Dragons, or you’ve never played a role‑playing game before, this edition is built to help you jump in quickly and start having fun.

For players who are curious about Pathfinder’s deeper rules, Darkness & Demons Basic also serves as a gentle bridge. Its three‑action economy, level‑based math, and simple proficiency system introduce the core ideas that make Pathfinder tick, but in a lighter, more forgiving form. Once you’re comfortable here, moving into a full Pathfinder game will feel natural and intuitive.

But this system is not just a stepping stone. Darkness & Demons Basic is a complete game in its own right, designed to stand on its own legs. If you enjoy the faster pace, the lighter rules, or the simplicity of play, you can keep adventuring in this system for as long as you like. Many groups will find that the clarity and ease of Basic are exactly what they want from a fantasy RPG—and there’s no need to go anywhere else unless you want to.

How to Use This Page

This webpage is organized to guide you from the basics of play to the full rules of the game in a clear, approachable order. The early chapters explain the core ideas you need to understand how the game works—rolling dice, making checks, taking actions, and leveling up. Later chapters introduce the options you can choose for your character, such as ancestries, classes, skills, equipment, and spells. The final chapters provide the rules for running adventures, resolving combat, and using monsters in your stories.

You do not need to read the entire ruleset before you start playing. Most groups begin by reading the Character Creation section, choosing an ancestry and class, and then learning the rules as they come up during play. The Game Master may want to read ahead into the Combat and GM Rules sections, but even that can be learned gradually. Darkness & Demons Basic is designed so that new players can jump in quickly and discover the game step by step.

Each section is written to stand on its own, so you can easily look up rules during play. Headings, clear examples, and consistent formatting make it simple to find what you need. The sidebar on the left lists every major section and subsection, allowing you to navigate the entire ruleset with a single click.

Whether you are learning your first tabletop role‑playing game or teaching it to others, this page is meant to be a friendly companion at the table. Read what you need, skip what you don’t, and return to any section whenever you want to explore more of what Darkness & Demons Basic has to offer.

Core Concepts

Darkness & Demons Basic is built on a few simple ideas that shape how the game plays. These concepts appear throughout the rules, and understanding them will help you make sense of everything from character abilities to combat encounters. The goal is not to memorize every detail, but to get a feel for how the game works and what your hero can do.

Tabletop role‑playing is cooperative storytelling. The Game Master describes the world and the challenges within it, while the players describe what their heroes attempt to do. The story moves forward through choices, imagination, and the roll of a twenty‑sided die. No two adventures are ever the same, because the players decide how to face each obstacle.

Most challenges are resolved with a d20 roll. When your hero attempts something uncertain—leaping a gap, recalling a spell, striking an enemy—you roll a d20 and add your modifiers. If your total meets or exceeds the Difficulty Class (DC), you succeed. This simple system keeps the game fast and easy to learn, while still allowing characters to grow stronger over time.

Your modifiers come from three places: your attributes, your proficiency, and your level. Attributes represent natural talent, proficiency shows training and experience, and your level reflects your overall growth as a hero. Darkness & Demons Basic uses a clean, predictable math system so you always know roughly how capable your character is at any task.

Deities are either holy or unholy. If a cleric's deity is holy, then they gain the healing font, while if their deityis unholy, they gain the harming font.

On your turn in combat, you get three actions. You can use these actions to move, attack, cast spells, or interact with the world. This flexible system lets you decide exactly how your hero acts each round without needing to memorize long lists of special rules. Most abilities and spells simply tell you how many actions they require.

As you gain levels, your hero becomes more capable. You learn new abilities, improve your skills, and gain access to stronger spells and equipment. Advancement is steady and straightforward, allowing new players to grow into the game at a comfortable pace while still offering meaningful choices.

Above all, Darkness & Demons Basic is about creativity, cooperation, and adventure. The rules exist to support your imagination, not to limit it. Use them as a foundation, explore boldly, and shape the story together with your group.

Dice & Rolls

Darkness & Demons Basic uses a small set of polyhedral dice to resolve actions and determine the outcomes of your hero’s choices. These dice are commonly written using the letter “d” followed by a number, such as d4 or d20. Each type of die is used for different purposes, but the twenty‑sided die—the d20—is the heart of the game.

The d20 is used for almost every important roll. Whenever your hero attempts something uncertain—attacking an enemy, leaping across a gap, recalling a piece of lore, or resisting a spell—you roll a d20 and add your modifiers. If your total meets or exceeds the Difficulty Class (DC), you succeed. This single, consistent mechanic keeps the game fast and easy to learn, and it applies to nearly every check you make during play.

Other dice are used mainly for damage and special effects. Weapons, spells, and certain abilities deal damage using smaller dice such as the d4, d6, d8, d10, or d12. These dice represent the swing of a sword, the blast of a spell, or the bite of a monster. Some abilities may also call for these dice to determine healing, random treasure, or other variable outcomes, but they are not used for checks or saving throws.

Rolling dice adds uncertainty and excitement to the story. Even the most skilled hero can miss a strike or fail a leap, and even the most fearsome monster can be brought low by a lucky blow. The dice ensure that every action carries risk and possibility, making each moment at the table unpredictable and memorable.

As long as you have a d20 and a few smaller dice for damage, you have everything you need to play. The rules will tell you which die to roll and when, and the rest is up to your hero’s choices—and a little luck.

How to Play

Checks & DCs

Whenever your hero attempts something uncertain, the game uses a simple system called a check to determine the outcome. A check represents your character trying to do something that could succeed or fail—swinging a weapon, climbing a wall, recalling a piece of lore, or resisting a spell. To make a check, you roll a twenty‑sided die (d20), add your modifiers, and compare the result to a target number called a Difficulty Class, or DC.

A check succeeds if your total meets or exceeds the DC. If your total is lower than the DC, the attempt fails. This single rule applies to almost every kind of roll in the game, keeping the system consistent and easy to learn. Attacks, skill uses, saving throws, and many special abilities all use this same structure.

In addition to simple success or failure, checks can also result in critical outcomes. If your total is 10 or more above the DC, you achieve a critical success, gaining an especially strong or impressive result. If your total is 10 or more below the DC, you suffer a critical failure, often making the situation worse. These outcomes add drama and unpredictability, turning ordinary rolls into memorable moments.

Your modifiers come from three places: your attribute modifier, your proficiency, and your level. Attributes represent natural talent, proficiency shows training and experience, and your level reflects your overall growth as a hero. Together, these numbers describe how capable your character is at a given task. For more about how skills work and how proficiency applies to them, see Skills.

Difficulty Classes represent how hard a task is. A low DC might reflect an easy jump or a simple piece of knowledge, while a high DC could represent a treacherous climb, a powerful spell, or a rare fact known only to experts. The Game Master sets DCs based on the situation, the environment, and the nature of the challenge. Most DCs fall within a predictable range, so players quickly learn what their characters are likely to succeed at.

Checks keep the game moving and the story dynamic. When the outcome of an action is uncertain, the roll of a d20 introduces tension, excitement, and possibility. Critical successes and failures heighten that tension, rewarding bold attempts and reminding players that even the simplest task can take an unexpected turn. With skill, preparation, and a little luck, heroes can overcome even the most daunting challenges.

Proficiency

Proficiency represents how skilled your hero is at a particular task. Every check you make—whether it involves a skill, an attack, or a saving throw—uses one of three proficiency levels: Untrained, Trained, or Expert. These levels determine how much of a bonus you add to your roll, reflecting your character’s experience and practice.

Untrained means you have no special training in that task. When you are untrained, you add your level to the roll, but you gain no proficiency bonus. You still add your attribute modifier, but otherwise you rely on natural talent or luck.

Trained characters have practiced the task enough to perform it reliably. When you are trained, you add a proficiency bonus of +2 plus your level. This represents both your focused learning and the general improvement that comes with adventuring experience.

Expert characters are highly skilled, capable of performing difficult tasks with confidence. When you are an expert, you add a proficiency bonus of +5 plus your level. This is the highest proficiency available in Darkness & Demons Basic, keeping the system simple while still allowing heroes to excel in their specialties.

Your total modifier for a check is your attribute modifier, plus your proficiency bonus (if any), plus your level if you are trained or expert. This predictable structure makes it easy to understand how capable your hero is at any moment, and it keeps the math consistent across the entire game.

Proficiency applies to skills, attacks, saving throws, and many class abilities. For more about how proficiency interacts with skills specifically, see Skills.

Attributes

Every hero in Darkness & Demons Basic is defined by six core attributes. These attributes represent natural talent, physical capability, and mental aptitude. Each attribute has a score, which determines its modifier. This modifier is added to many of your checks, attacks, and other rolls throughout the game. Higher scores mean stronger abilities, while lower scores represent weaknesses or challenges your hero must overcome.

Strength (STR) measures physical power, athletic ability, and how hard you can hit. Strength is important for melee attacks, climbing, lifting, breaking objects, and many physical challenges. Characters who rely on weapons or brute force often value a high Strength score.

Dexterity (DEX) represents agility, reflexes, balance, and precision. Dexterity affects ranged attacks, dodging danger, sneaking, picking locks, and reacting quickly to threats. It also influences your Armor Class, making it vital for nimble or lightly armored heroes.

Constitution (CON) reflects endurance, toughness, and overall health. Constitution determines your hit points and helps you resist poison, disease, and exhaustion. A high Constitution makes your hero more durable and able to withstand the dangers of adventuring.

Intelligence (INT) measures reasoning, memory, and knowledge. Intelligence affects your ability to recall information, understand complex subjects, and solve puzzles. Wizards and other scholarly characters rely on Intelligence for their spellcasting and academic skills.

Wisdom (WIS) represents perception, intuition, willpower, and awareness of the world. Wisdom helps you notice hidden details, resist mental influence, and understand the intentions of others. Clerics and druids use Wisdom as the foundation of their spellcasting.

Charisma (CHA) reflects presence, confidence, and the ability to influence others. Charisma is used for persuasion, intimidation, leadership, and social interactions. It also fuels certain magical traditions and abilities tied to force of personality.

Together, these six attributes form the backbone of your hero’s abilities. They shape how you approach challenges, what your character excels at, and how you grow over time. Many checks and actions in the game will call for one of these attributes, and your modifier plays a key role in determining success.

Level & Advancement

Heroes in Darkness & Demons Basic grow stronger by gaining experience points, or XP. As characters overcome challenges, defeat monsters, complete quests, and explore dangerous places, they earn XP that reflects what they have learned along the way. When a hero gains enough XP, they advance to the next level, improving their abilities and unlocking new powers.

The game has 20 levels of advancement. Every character begins at 1st level and can progress all the way to 20th level over the course of their adventures. Each new level represents a meaningful step forward in skill, confidence, and capability. Characters gain hit points, improve their proficiency, and may learn new class features or spells depending on their class.

It takes 1,000 XP to gain a level. This requirement is the same at every stage of play: going from 1st to 2nd level requires 1,000 XP, and going from 19th to 20th level also requires 1,000 XP. This simple structure keeps advancement predictable and easy to track, especially for new players and groups who prefer a straightforward progression system.

The details of how XP is awarded—including guidelines for encounters, exploration, treasure, and story accomplishments—are described in the GM Rules section. Game Masters may follow those guidelines closely or adjust them to suit the style and pace of their campaign.

Leveling up is one of the most exciting parts of the game. Each new level represents your hero’s growing legend, marking the dangers they have survived and the challenges they are ready to face next.

Character Creation

Steps of Creation

Creating a hero in Darkness & Demons Basic is a simple, step‑by‑step process. Each step helps you shape your character’s identity, abilities, and place in the world. You can complete these steps in order, or move back and forth between them as inspiration strikes. By the end, you will have a fully prepared adventurer ready to explore dungeons, battle monsters, and take part in heroic stories.

1. Choose an Ancestry. Your ancestry represents your character’s heritage and natural traits. Each ancestry provides unique abilities, attribute adjustments, and special features that help define who your hero is. See Ancestries for details.

2. Choose a Class. Your class determines your role in the party and the abilities you gain as you advance. Fighters excel in combat, rogues rely on agility and skill, wizards wield arcane power, clerics channel divine magic, druids source their magic from melding with nature, and barbarians embrace their rage to inflict lethal wounds. Each class offers a distinct playstyle. See Classes for more information.

3. Assign Your Attributes. Your six attributes—Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma—shape your hero’s natural talents. Your ancestry and class may guide your choices, but you are free to build the character you imagine. See Attributes for descriptions of each ability.

4. Select Your Skills. Skills represent training and experience in specific areas such as athletics, stealth, or knowledge. Your class grants certain proficiencies, and you may choose additional skills to reflect your background or personality. See Skills for the full list.

5. Choose Your Equipment. Every hero begins with basic gear, weapons, and armor. Your class provides starting options, and you may customize your loadout to suit your style. See Equipment for details on weapons, armor, and adventuring gear.

6. Record Your Hit Points and Defenses. Your class and Constitution determine your starting hit points, while your Armor Class and saving throws depend on your attributes, proficiency, and equipment. These numbers help define how durable your hero is in dangerous situations.

7. Add Background Details. Give your hero a name, appearance, personality, and a brief history. These details help bring your character to life and guide your decisions during play.

8. Begin Your Adventure. Once your character sheet is complete, you are ready to join the party and start exploring the world. As you play, you will gain experience, grow stronger, and shape your hero’s story through your choices and actions.

Choosing an Ancestry

Your character’s ancestry represents their heritage, natural abilities, and the culture they come from. It provides a few starting traits and helps shape your hero’s identity, but it does not determine their destiny or limit the class you can choose. Pick the ancestry that best matches the kind of character you want to play—whether that means following familiar fantasy archetypes or imagining something entirely your own. For full details on each option, see Ancestries.

Choosing a Class

Your class defines your hero’s role, abilities, and style of play. It determines how you fight, what special powers you gain, and how your character grows from level to level. Choose the class that best matches the kind of hero you want to become—whether that means a skilled warrior, a clever rogue, a devoted cleric, or a master of magic. Every class in this book is fully playable from 1st to 20th level, so pick the one that inspires you. For full details on each option, see Classes.

Starting Equipment

Every hero begins the game with a set of starting equipment chosen from the options provided by their class. This gear includes basic weapons, armor, and adventuring supplies that reflect your character’s training and role. Starting equipment ensures that all characters begin play ready for action without needing to track gold or make complex purchasing decisions. As you advance in your adventures, you will find treasure, upgrade your gear, and customize your loadout to suit your style. For the specific items each class begins with, see Classes.

Assigning Attributes

Your hero’s attributes begin with a set of boosts that shape their natural strengths and weaknesses. During character creation, you gain four free attribute boosts. Each boost increases an attribute by +1, but you cannot apply more than one free boost to the same attribute. These boosts represent your character’s personal talents and early training.

Your ancestry also provides attribute adjustments. Most ancestries grant one or more boosts and may include a flaw that reduces an attribute by –1. Ancestries also grant a free boost, which can be to the same attribute as one of your other free boosts. These adjustments reflect the natural traits of your character’s heritage. Apply your ancestry’s boosts and flaws before your free boosts unless the ancestry specifies otherwise. For full details, see Ancestries.

After applying your ancestry adjustments and your four free boosts, your six attributes—Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma—are ready to use. These values determine your modifiers, which you will add to many checks throughout the game.

Hit Points & Defenses

Your hero’s durability in battle is represented by their hit points, or HP. To calculate your starting HP, add together three values: your ancestry’s HP, your class’s HP, and your Constitution modifier. This total represents how tough your character is at 1st level. Each time you gain a level, you add your class’s HP and your Constitution modifier again.

Armor Class (AC) measures how difficult you are to hit in combat. Your AC is calculated as 10 + your armor’s bonus + your Dexterity modifier if you are wearing light armor. Medium and heavy armor limit how much Dexterity you can apply, and shields can provide additional bonuses. Your AC represents your ability to avoid or deflect incoming attacks.

Saving throws represent your hero’s ability to resist harmful effects. Darkness & Demons Basic uses three saves: Fortitude is based on Constitution and represents endurance and physical resilience, Reflex is based on Dexterity and represents speed and agility, and Will is based on Wisdom and represents mental strength and resolve. Each class begins trained in one saving throw and untrained in the other two. As you advance in level, your proficiency bonus increases, improving your defenses over time.

Ancestries

Human

Humans are adaptable, ambitious, and endlessly varied. Their cultures span every corner of the world, and no two human communities are quite alike. Their versatility makes them capable of excelling in any role or profession.

Hit Points: 8 HP

Attribute Boosts: Two free

Attribute Flaw: —

Size: Medium

Speed: 25 feet

Vision: Normal

Elf

Elves are graceful, perceptive, and long‑lived, with a deep connection to magic and the natural world. Their keen senses and disciplined minds make them thoughtful explorers, skilled hunters, and powerful spellcasters.

Hit Points: 6 HP

Attribute Boosts: Dexterity, Free

Attribute Flaw: Constitution

Size: Medium

Speed: 30 feet

Vision: Low-Light

Dwarf

Dwarves are sturdy, determined, and proud of their ancient traditions. Known for their resilience and craftsmanship, dwarves excel in harsh environments and stand unshaken against danger, both physical and magical.

Hit Points: 10 HP

Attribute Boosts: Constitution, Free

Attribute Flaw: Charisma

Size: Medium

Speed: 20 feet

Vision: Darkvision

Halfling

Halflings are small, nimble, and remarkably brave, guided by optimism and an unshakable sense of community. Their quick reflexes and steady hearts make them reliable companions and surprisingly bold adventurers.

Hit Points: 8 HP

Attribute Boosts: Wisdom, Free

Attribute Flaw: Strength

Size: Small

Speed: 25 feet

Vision: Low-Light

Goblin

Goblins are quick, curious, and fiercely energetic, driven by a mix of mischief and ingenuity. Though often underestimated, goblins are clever survivors with sharp instincts and a knack for turning chaos into opportunity.

Hit Points: 6 HP

Attribute Boosts: Dexterity, Charisma, Free

Attribute Flaws: Wisdom, Strength

Size: Small

Speed: 25 feet

Vision: Darkvision

Orc

Orcs are strong, fierce, and driven by a powerful sense of purpose. Their endurance and warrior spirit are matched by deep cultural bonds, and many orcs channel their strength into honor, discipline, and determination.

Hit Points: 12 HP

Attribute Boosts: Strength, Free

Attribute Flaw: Intelligence

Size: Medium

Speed: 25 feet

Vision: Low-Light

Classes

Fighter

Fighters are disciplined warriors who excel in direct combat. They rely on skill, training, and physical prowess to control the battlefield, adapt to any weapon, and stand firm against overwhelming odds.

Hit Points: 10

Key Attribute: Strength or Dexterity

Proficiencies:

  • Saving Throws: Trained in Fortitude or Reflex, Untrained in the other two.
  • Weapons: Trained in all weapons.
  • Armor: Trained in all armor.
  • Skills: Trained in Athletics, Acrobatics, and additional skills equal to your Intelligence modifier. See Skills.

Starting Equipment: Choose from the options listed under this class. See Equipment for item details.

  • Two weapons of your choice
  • Light or medium armor
  • A shield
  • An adventurer's pack
  • 5 gold

Class Features:

  • Reactive Strike — 1st — You gain a the Reactive Strike feat, ignoring the level restriction.
  • Martial Training — 2nd — You gain a 1st- or 2nd-level feat with the Martial trait, for which you meet the prerequisites.
  • Weapon Expertise — 4th — You gain Expert proficiency with all weapons.
  • Battle Hardened — 6th — When you get a success on a Fortitude save, it counts as a critical success.
  • Attribute Boosts — 8th — You can increase three attributes of your choice by 1 each. You cannot choose the same attribute twice.
  • Weapon Specialization — 10th — You deal an additional die of damage when you hit with a weapon. For example, 2d8 instead of 1d8.
  • Attribute Boosts — 13th — You can increase three attributes of your choice by 1 each. You cannot choose the same attribute twice.
  • Defensive Mind and Body — 16th — Your proficiency rank for the saves in which you are untraind increases to Trained.
  • Combat Mastery — 18th — You gain Expert proficiency with all armor, and you deal an additional die of damage when you hit with a weapon.
  • Unbreakable — 20th — You gain Expert proficiency in Fortitude or Reflex saves. In addition, your maximum HP increases by 20.

Advancement: You may choose a feat whose level equals your level or under at levels 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 12, 14, 15, 17, and 19.

Rogue

Rogues are agile, cunning, and resourceful, thriving on precision and quick thinking. They excel at stealth, trickery, and exploiting openings, striking where enemies are weakest.

Hit Points: 8

Key Attribute: Dexterity

Proficiencies:

  • Saving Throws: Trained in Reflex, Untrained in the other two.
  • Weapons: Trained in daggers, rapiers, crossbows, and shortswords.
  • Armor: Trained in light armor.
  • Skills: Trained in Stealth, Acrobatics, Thievery, and additional skills equal to your Intelligence modifier plus 3. See Skills.

Starting Equipment: Choose from the options listed under this class. See Equipment for item details.

  • Two weapons in which you are trained
  • Leather armor
  • An adventurer's pack
  • 5 gold

Class Features:

  • Sneak Attack — 1st — When you hit an off-guard creature, you deal an additional 1d6 damage.
  • Skill Increase — 2nd — You become trained in one skill in which you're untrained.
  • Surprise Attack — 4th — All creatures who have not yet taken a turn in combat are off-guard to you.
  • Improved Sneak Attack — 6th — Your Sneak Attack damage increases to 2d6.
  • Attribute Boosts — 8th — You can increase three attributes of your choice by 1 each. You cannot choose the same attribute twice.
  • Evasive Reflexes — 10th — When you get a success on a Reflex save, it counts as a critical success.
  • Attribute Boosts — 13th — You can increase three attributes of your choice by 1 each. You cannot choose the same attribute twice.
  • Skill Finesse — 16th — You gain 3 skill increases, each one of which you can use to increase your proficiency rank in a single skill by one step.
  • Physical Perfection — 18th — Your proficiency rank for Reflex saves increases to Expert, and to Trained for Fortitude.
  • Skill Mastery — 20th — Your profiency rank for all skills increases by one step. If you were already an expert in a skill, you add an additional +1.

Advancement: You may choose a feat whose level equals your level or under at levels 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 12, 15, 17, and 19.

Using Skills

Skills represent the things your character knows how to do — climbing walls, recalling lore, sneaking through shadows, treating wounds, and much more. When you attempt something risky or uncertain, the GM may call for a skill check. You roll a d20, add the relevant attribute modifier and your proficiency bonus, and compare the result to the Difficulty Class (DC). If the outcome is obvious or failure wouldn’t matter, no roll is needed; the action simply succeeds. Skills help define your character’s strengths and give you ways to interact with the world beyond combat.

Acrobatics: Balancing on narrow surfaces, tumbling past foes, escaping restraints, and performing agile stunts.

Arcana: Identifying magical effects, understanding arcane theory, recognizing spells, and recalling knowledge about magical creatures.

Athletics: Climbing, swimming, jumping, shoving, grappling, and performing feats of physical strength.

Deception: Telling convincing lies, creating distractions, disguising your intentions, and misleading others.

Diplomacy: Negotiating, persuading, calming tensions, gathering information through conversation, and making a good impression.

Intimidation: Using forceful presence, threats, or commanding tone to influence others or frighten opponents.

Lore: Recalling specialized knowledge about a particular subject, profession, region, or field of study.

Medicine: Treating wounds, stabilizing dying creatures, diagnosing ailments, and providing basic long‑term care.

Nature: Understanding plants, animals, weather, and natural hazards, as well as identifying primal magic and creatures.

Performance: Singing, acting, dancing, storytelling, or otherwise entertaining an audience with practiced skill.

Religion: Recognizing divine magic, understanding deities and rituals, recalling religious lore, and identifying undead.

Society: Knowing customs, laws, politics, urban environments, and the workings of civilization and culture.

Stealth: Moving quietly, hiding from sight, avoiding detection, and slipping away unnoticed.

Survival: Tracking creatures, navigating the wilderness, predicting weather, foraging, and enduring harsh environments.

Thievery: Picking locks, disabling traps, palm­ing objects, and performing delicate or illicit manual tasks.

Skill Proficiency

Each skill on your character sheet has a proficiency level that shows how capable you are when using it. When you attempt a skill check, you add the attribute modifier for that skill, plus your proficiency bonus if you are Trained or Expert. If you are Untrained, you rely only on your attribute and your level.

Untrained characters can still attempt most skill checks, but they do so with no proficiency bonus. This represents basic, everyday ability without formal practice.

Trained characters add a +2 proficiency bonus, reflecting focused study or hands‑on experience. A trained hero can handle most routine challenges in their area of expertise.

Expert characters add a +5 proficiency bonus, marking them as highly skilled. Experts can attempt difficult tasks with confidence and often achieve exceptional results.

Your skill proficiencies help define your hero’s strengths—whether they excel at climbing, negotiating, recalling lore, or moving unseen. As you advance in level, your proficiency bonuses grow with you, ensuring your specialties remain meaningful throughout your adventures.
